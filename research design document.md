# Research Design: Robust Course-of-Action Generation Through Adversarial Self-Play

## 1. Research Goal

The goal of this research is to determine whether multi-agent, adversarial self-play can generate Courses of Action (COAs) that remain effective against adaptive opponents, rather than only performing well against a fixed scenario or a passive evaluator.

The project treats COA planning as a two-player game. A BLUE planning agent proposes a COA, a RED agent generates a best response, and BLUE iteratively revises its policy. A Mission Effectiveness Function (MEF) evaluates individual COAs in terms of effectiveness, cost, and risk. A Game-Based COA (GBC) evaluation compares opposing COAs and measures performance under adversarial interaction.

All experiments use synthetic scenarios and simulated assets. The system is intended as a research and decision-support benchmark, not as an autonomous operational planning system.

## 2. Research Objective

The primary objective is to test whether LLM-guided best-response self-play produces COAs that are more robust to unseen adversarial responses than COAs generated by random, heuristic, greedy, or non-adversarial LLM baselines.

The secondary objectives are to:

1. Evaluate whether MEF and GBC provide useful, complementary measures of COA quality.
2. Measure the tradeoff between mission effectiveness, cost, risk, and COA diversity.
3. Identify when self-play improves planning and when it overfits, cycles, collapses to repetitive strategies, or exploits weaknesses in the simulator or evaluator.
4. Produce a reproducible multi-agent simulation and evaluation artifact suitable for comparing future COA-generation methods.

## 3. Research Questions

**RQ1.** Does best-response self-play improve worst-case performance against held-out adversaries?

**RQ2.** Does LLM guidance improve self-play relative to random or heuristic candidate generation?

**RQ3.** Are improvements robust across scenario types, force balances, uncertainty levels, and random seeds?

**RQ4.** Does the method preserve useful COA diversity, or does repeated self-play converge to a narrow set of actions?

**RQ5.** What are the computational cost, reliability, and failure modes of using LLM agents in the planning loop?

## 4. Hypotheses

**H1. Robustness:** LLM-guided best-response self-play will achieve a higher held-out worst-case GBC score than every baseline.

**H2. Average performance:** The proposed method will achieve a higher mean GBC score and win rate against a mixed population of adversaries than non-adversarial LLM generation.

**H3. Generalization:** Improvements will persist on scenarios, force compositions, and opponent policies not used during self-play.

**H4. Ablation:** Removing either adversarial best-response generation or iterative policy improvement will reduce robustness.

**H5. Diversity tradeoff:** Unconstrained self-play may reduce COA diversity; diversity-aware candidate selection will retain more distinct strategies without materially reducing robustness.

These are hypotheses to be tested. They must not be reported as findings until the experiments have been completed.

## 5. Proposed System

The system consists of five separable components:

1. **Scenario generator:** Produces synthetic game states, objectives, assets, constraints, uncertainty, and force balances.
2. **COA generator:** Produces candidate action chains using random, heuristic, greedy, or LLM-guided policies.
3. **Best-response agent:** Generates an opposing COA intended to exploit weaknesses in a candidate plan.
4. **Simulator:** Applies both COAs to the same state using explicit and seeded transition rules.
5. **Evaluator:** Computes MEF, GBC, robustness, diversity, constraint violations, runtime, and failure statistics.

The components must have stable interfaces so that generation quality can be evaluated independently from simulator dynamics and scoring assumptions.

## 6. Experimental Scenarios

Experiments should cover several synthetic scenario families rather than one demonstration scenario:

- Contested movement or maneuver
- Defense of a fixed objective
- Resource-constrained logistics and resupply
- Information gathering under uncertainty
- Cyber defense and service restoration

Each family should vary:

- BLUE/RED force balance
- asset capability and availability
- resource budget
- observation uncertainty
- action failure probability
- scenario horizon
- adversary aggressiveness

Scenario templates should be split into training/development and held-out test sets. Test templates and seeds must not be included in prompts or self-play training episodes.

## 7. Baseline Experiments

All methods receive the same initial state, objective, action schema, candidate budget, simulator, and evaluation budget.

### B0: Random Valid COA

Sample valid actions and priorities uniformly from the available action space. This establishes a lower-bound performance level and checks whether the task is non-trivial.

### B1: Greedy MEF

Generate candidates and select the COA with the highest estimated MEF without modelling an opponent. This tests whether optimizing a single-plan score is sufficient.

### B2: Scripted Heuristic Planner

Use domain rules to produce a valid sequence such as observe, prepare, act, and recover. This represents a transparent, inexpensive planning baseline.

### B3: LLM Generation Without Self-Play

Ask the same LLM used by the proposed method to produce a COA once, with no RED response or iterative revision. This isolates the value of adversarial self-play from the value of the language model.

### B4: Random or Heuristic Self-Play

Run the same iterative self-play structure using a non-LLM generator. This isolates the value of LLM guidance from the value of self-play itself.

## 8. Test Experiment

The principal treatment is **LLM-guided best-response self-play**.

For each scenario:

1. BLUE generates a population of candidate COAs.
2. RED generates one or more best responses to each candidate.
3. The simulator evaluates each BLUE/RED pairing.
4. BLUE retains or revises candidates using GBC and robustness scores.
5. Steps 2-4 repeat for a fixed number of rounds or until improvement stops.
6. The final BLUE policy is frozen.
7. The frozen policy is evaluated against held-out opponents that were not used during self-play.

The primary comparison is:

> LLM-guided best-response self-play versus B0-B4 on held-out scenarios and held-out adversary policies.

Using held-out opponents is essential. Evaluation only against self-play opponents would risk measuring adaptation to a particular RED policy rather than general robustness.

## 9. Ablation Experiments

The following ablations identify which components cause any observed improvement:

- Remove best-response generation and use fixed RED behavior.
- Remove iterative revision and retain the initial LLM COA.
- Replace GBC selection with MEF-only selection.
- Replace the LLM with random candidate generation.
- Remove action-chain dependencies and use flat action lists.
- Remove diversity-aware selection.
- Vary self-play rounds and candidate population size.
- Vary the LLM or inference temperature while holding prompts fixed.

## 10. Evaluation Metrics

### Primary Metric

**Held-out robustness:** the lower-tail or worst-case GBC score against a set of unseen adversarial policies. A lower quantile, such as the 10th percentile, should be reported alongside the strict minimum because a single simulator outlier can make the minimum unstable.

### Secondary Metrics

- Mean and median GBC score
- Win, loss, and draw rate
- MEF effectiveness, cost, and risk components
- Regret or exploitability against the strongest discovered response
- Performance on held-out scenario families
- COA action and structural diversity
- Invalid-action and constraint-violation rate
- LLM/tool failure and retry rate
- Runtime, model calls, token use, and estimated inference cost
- Stability across random seeds

The current `nash_gap` metric should only be called a Nash or exploitability measure if it is derived from valid game payoffs and best-response calculations. Payoff complementarity alone is not evidence of convergence to a Nash equilibrium.

## 11. Experimental Controls and Analysis

- Use at least 30 independent seeds per major condition where computationally feasible.
- Pair methods on identical scenarios and seeds.
- Give every method the same candidate and simulator-query budget.
- Freeze prompts, model versions, decoding parameters, and simulator configuration before the final evaluation.
- Report confidence intervals and paired effect sizes, not only means.
- Use a paired bootstrap or an appropriate paired non-parametric test when score distributions are non-normal.
- Correct for multiple comparisons when testing several methods or scenario families.
- Publish per-scenario results so aggregate scores do not hide failure cases.

## 12. Expected Result Patterns

No numerical result should be assumed before running the experiments. The study is designed to distinguish among these plausible outcomes:

### Outcome A: Self-Play Improves Robustness

The proposed method has a meaningfully higher lower-tail GBC score than the baselines while maintaining acceptable cost, validity, and diversity. This would support the claim that adversarial iteration improves robust planning.

### Outcome B: LLM Quality, Not Self-Play, Explains the Gain

LLM self-play and one-shot LLM generation perform similarly. This would show that the model is useful, but the self-play contribution requires revision.

### Outcome C: Self-Play Overfits

Training-opponent scores improve while held-out-opponent scores remain flat or decline. This would be an important negative result about opponent diversity and evaluation design.

### Outcome D: Simple Methods Are Competitive

Greedy or scripted baselines match the proposed method at much lower cost. This would narrow the circumstances in which LLM-guided self-play is justified.

### Outcome E: Evaluator Exploitation

MEF or GBC improves while plans become invalid, repetitive, or unrealistic. This would reveal metric gaming and motivate stronger constraints, human review, or multi-objective evaluation.

## 13. Why the Research Matters

Many planning systems evaluate a plan in isolation or against a fixed opponent. That can reward brittle plans that fail as soon as another agent adapts. A reproducible adversarial benchmark would make robustness measurable and would help separate attractive-looking plans from plans that survive strategic response.

The work matters scientifically because it connects LLM agents, multi-agent learning, game-theoretic evaluation, and sequential planning. It also provides an empirical setting for studying opponent overfitting, evaluator exploitation, coordination failure, and the cost-benefit tradeoffs of agentic self-play.

The work matters practically because organizations considering multi-agent decision-support systems need evidence about reliability, oversight, operating cost, and failure modes, not only demonstrations of fluent plan generation. A modular and synthetic benchmark lets researchers study these questions without requiring classified, proprietary, or operational data.

## 14. Why People Would Care

- **Multi-agent researchers** receive a benchmark for strategic generation and adaptive evaluation.
- **LLM-agent researchers** receive evidence about whether iterative adversarial reasoning adds value over one-shot prompting.
- **Simulation and planning practitioners** receive reusable metrics, scenario interfaces, and failure analyses.
- **Industry teams** receive implementation lessons about observability, reproducibility, cost, and human oversight.
- **Safety and governance researchers** receive a controlled environment for studying deceptive behavior, escalation, metric gaming, and intervention points.

The broader contribution should be framed as robust multi-agent decision-support research. Claims should remain limited to synthetic simulation unless validated through an appropriately governed real-world study.

## 15. Threats to Validity

- Simulator assumptions may predetermine which policies succeed.
- MEF weights may encode subjective preferences.
- An LLM may exploit textual or scoring artifacts rather than reason strategically.
- Synthetic scenarios may not transfer to real environments.
- A small opponent population may overstate robustness.
- Model updates may make exact replication difficult.
- Stochastic results may be sensitive to prompts, seeds, and inference settings.

These risks should be addressed through sensitivity analysis, held-out opponents, multiple scenario families, explicit versioning, ablations, and transparent release of configurations and raw results.

## 16. Minimum Evidence for the Paper

Before making the central robustness claim, the project should provide:

1. A deterministic simulator with documented transition rules.
2. At least four meaningful baselines, including one-shot LLM generation.
3. Held-out scenarios and adversary policies.
4. A complete ablation of LLM guidance, best response, self-play, and scoring.
5. Confidence intervals across independent seeds.
6. Runtime and inference-cost reporting.
7. Qualitative analysis of successful plans and major failure cases.
8. A reproducibility command that regenerates the principal tables and figures.
9. A limitations, ethics, and safeguards section appropriate for a high-impact domain.
